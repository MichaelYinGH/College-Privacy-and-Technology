Introduction to De-Anonymization

An introduction to the problem of data de-anonymization and its privacy implications

Why Collect and Publish Data?

With the growing prevalence of the internet and its multitude of online services, large datasets containing potentially private information are plentiful.

Still, the collection and transmission of such data is often necessary or at least beneficial.

For instance, technology companies may rely on user data to see what features are popular, or academic researchers may use such data to analyze human behavior.

Anonymizing Data

Oftentimes, potentially sensitive data is anonymized, or de-identified, so that it can be shared with fewer privacy concerns.

This involves removing directly identifying information, such as name or email.

De-Anonymizing Data

However, even anonymized data may still contain clues that can be used to piece back together individual identities.

Sensically, this process is known as de-anonymization, or re-identification.

Matching Datasets

There are several large or well-known instances of de-anonymization.

For instance, University of Texas at Austin researchers matched ratings from anonymized Netflix data with Internet Movie Database rankings to find out who many of the Netflix accounts belonged to.

Being Aware

Even if your data will be anonymized, be careful when giving out personal information!

Large institutions can't always perfectly prevent de-anonymization, much less individual's surveys.

Think about what information you would and wouldn't be okay with putting out there if it were to be traced back to you.
