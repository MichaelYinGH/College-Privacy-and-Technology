The College Student's Guide to Privacy and Technology

Ten 1-minute things you can do to protect your privacy and security

(1) Encrypt your hard drive

What if somebody seized or stole your laptop? It is possible, although unlikely, that your laptop could be seized and searched when passing through security while traveling, entering a venue, etc. To prevent individuals from accessing your data without your consent, you can quickly and easily encrypt your hard drive that will only unlock if you enter a password.
For a macOS laptop: System Preferences → Security & Privacy → FileVault → Turn on FileVault if it’s not automatically enabled.
For a Windows laptop: Start button > Settings > Update & Security > Device encryption. If there is no “Device Encryption” option, click “Manage Bitlocker” and turn the feature on.


(2) Install an antivirus program

Are you worried about viruses other than the coronavirus? You should be! Antivirus software defends against computer viruses, ransomware, and Trojan horse programs, all of which can be easily encountered while casually browsing the internet. Harvard Internet Security recommends using either BitDefender Antivirus Free or Avast Free Antivirus. For more details, click here.

(3) Use a password vault

Do you use the same password across dozens of sites? Ideally, all of your passwords should be distinct and somewhat complex. Instead of attempting to remember each of your passwords, use a single secure password vault instead. Lastpass offers very convenient and easy to use options such as autofilling username and password at most sites. Free Lastpass Premium for Harvard affiliates can be found here and general free Lastpass Premium for students can be found here.

(4) Turn on 2-factor authentication for your most protected accounts

Your most important accounts, such as your bank account or your password vault, should be under extra protection in addition to the password stored in your password vault detailed above. There are three main types of authentication: something you know (typically a password), something you have (usually your phone), and something you are (biometrics such as your fingerprint). If you navigate to the privacy settings of most accounts, there is often an option to add a “something you have” authentication using Duo mobile or other methods. Adding this option makes it less likely that individuals can access your account in the case of a password leak.

(5) Get a VPN and use it on less secure networks

Public wifi, such as WiFi at a coffee shop, is less secure than private wifi. To better secure your privacy, use a VPN, which extends a private network across a public network. Harvard offers the use of Cisco AnyConnect Secure to anyone with a HarvardKey. Instructions can be found here.

(6) Set up Mobile Pay and use it as much as possible

Mobile pay methods, such as Apple Pay, are often both more convenient and more secure. Instead of sending over your full credit card information, as you do with your physical credit or debit card, mobile pay methods generate a one-time authentication code for each transaction, making it impossible for someone to re-use that information to conduct another transaction. Setting up mobile pay usually only involves scanning your credit card and takes less than a minute. In addition, after initially entering in your credit card information, your smartphone does not keep your full credit card information stored on your device.
On an iPhone: open the Wallet app, tap the add button, and follow the instructions to enter your credit card information (Instructions).
On an Android: Open the Google Pay app, tap Menu on the top left, select Payment methods, select Enable contactless, and then follow the instructions to verify your payment method (Instructions).

(7) Look up your email addresses and phone numbers on haveibeenpwned.com to see if your information has been leaked or compromised

Information such as passwords, IP addresses, and other private information is often exposed through leaks over the years. Many people share passwords between accounts and can easily leak passwords for multiple accounts in one leak, so be sure to take a look to see if your information was leaked!

(8) Use a privacy-preserving search engine, such as DuckDuckGo, instead of Google

Google, Bing, and other search engines collect your previous searches, what results you click on, etc. and link that data to your IP address and account in order to advertise specifically to you. This information collection can be prevented by using a privacy conscious search engine such as DuckDuckGo, which records anonymized search history but does not retain your IP address or other identifying information.
To change your default search history on Google Chrome: Click on the three vertical dots in top right corner, by your Chrome extensions → Settings → Search Engine → Change dropdown from Google to DuckDuckGo
